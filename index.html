About Me

"To discover, understand, and cure -- all through data." says Andy.
My name is Yuzhe Chen, and I am also known as Andy. I am a graduate student at Tsinghua University, passionate about learning and analysis of biomedical data, especially neuroscience data. My motivation comes from the desire to understand the mechanism of the human mind and body, as well as the longing to care for myself and others, and neuroscience helps me to achieve such purposes. My research analyzes dynamic brain connectivity using statistical learning and signal processing methods, through which I want to model the time-varying brain topology. I have been working on discovering the brain transition patterns and statistical metrics of neurological disorders.
Besides research, I also like sports, music, learning languages, and meeting people from diverse backgrounds.

Projects

Project 1: Time-varying brain state transition analysis using physics-inspired graph measures

Employed a sliding window approach to construct time-varying brain networks.
Analyzed dynamic functional connectivity by importing concepts from thermodynamics.
Uncovered brain state transition mechanisms by identifying outliers and extreme values in thermodynamic quantity measures.
Tested the correlation between temperature spikes and brain state transitions and verified the statistical significance of such correlation.

Project 2: Dynamic Functional Connectivity Analysis using Topological and Graph Theoretical Methods

Processed EEG and fMRI data of neurological disorders, including schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, autism spectrum disorder, etc.
Applied graph theory and topological data analysis to extract brain state networks and study temporal brain state organization.
Studied graph/simplicial spectral features and statistical measures of the dynamic connectivity of neurological disorders.
Examined differences in distributions of brain temporal statistics for different subject groups.

Project 3: Cancer Gene Expression Data Analysis using Graph Signal Processing

Applied graph signal processing and graph frequency filtering techniques to extract cancer gene expression features.
Employed dimension reduction and clustering algorithms (Gaussian Mixture, Spectral Clustering, K-Means, etc.) to model subject data.
The Gaussian Mixture binary clustering accuracy reached higher than 97% for control vs. lung/stomach/colon cancer groups.
